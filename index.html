How to get vaccinated in Maryland:



Maryland Vaccine Information

Who can get vaccinated:

Maryland entered Phase 2B on March 30, 2021. Maryland will move on to Phase 2C on April 13, which includes Marylanders age 55 and over and an expanded list of essential workers. Governor Larry Hogan said that by April 27, Maryland will make the vaccine available to everyone in the state age 16 and older. Preregistration for all phases is now available.

Maryland is currently in Phase 2B. This means that the following people are eligible to be vaccinated:



PHASE 1A

  • All licensed, registered and certified healthcare providers.
  • Nursing home residents and staff.
  • Frontline hospital staff.
  • Law enforcement.
  • Firefighters.
  • EMS personnel.
  • Correctional healthcare staff and officers.
  • Frontline judiciary staff.
  • Continuity of government workers.

PHASE 1B

  • Anyone 75 and over.
  • Anyone in assisted living, independent living, and other congregate facilities.
  • Individuals with intellectual and developmental disabilities.
  • High-risk incarcerated individuals.
  • Continuity of government workers.
  • Education staff, including K-12 teachers, support staff, and daycare providers.

PHASE 1C

  • Anyone 65 and over.
  • All public safety workers.
  • All healthcare workers.
  • Frontline judiciary staff.
  • Grocery store workers.
  • U.S.P.S. workers.
  • Agricultural production workers.
  • Public mass transit workers.
  • Critical manufacturing workers.
  • Veterinarians and veterinary support staff.
  • Clergy and other essential support for houses of worship.

PHASE 2A

  • Anyone 60 and over became eligible on March 23.

PHASE 2B

  • Anyone 16 and older with underlying medical conditions that can increase the risk for severe COVID-19 illness will be eligble starting March 31.
  • Marylanders ages 16+ with disabilities who are receiving SSI or SSDI benefits, Maryland Medicaid EID individuals, Maryland Medicaid REM recipients, and Marylanders receiving TDAP benefits.
  • Marylanders receiving long term services and support through the state’s Medicaid waiver and state plan services.

The following groups are not yet eligible for the vaccine, but will be soon based on the Maryland state governement timeline. Click through the remaining groups to see who is eligible when.

PHASE 2C

  • Anyone 55 and over, construction, food service, utilities, transportation, financial services, IT and other infrastructure workers will be eligble starting April 13.

PHASE 3

  • The general population, including healthy adults age 16 and over, will be eligible starting April 27.

Frequently asked questions:


What is the COVID-19 vaccine?

  • When a person is infected with the virus that causes COVID-19, their body takes several weeks to develop different types of white blood cells that fight off the disease, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. Over the course of a few days or weeks, the body tries to fight off the infection by developing an immune response to COVID-19 germs. If the infection is defeated, the body keeps some “memory” cells that remember how it fought the disease, which allow it to quickly spring into action if the person is infected again.
  • Vaccines prepare the body’s immune system to fight off disease without needing that experience. A COVID-19 vaccination includes these “memory” cells (along with some others) that help the body fight off COVID-19.
  • Just like the immune system needs time to build immunity while fighting COVID-19, the body needs time to build immunity after getting a COVID-19 vaccination. This means even those who get the vaccine may need to wait some time before they are protected against the virus.

How many doses of the vaccine will I need?

  • One currently authorized COVID-19 vaccine, manufactured by Janssen (Johnson & Johnson), requires a single dose.
  • Two of the currently authorized COVID-19 vaccines require two shots for optimal effectiveness, according to the CDC.
    • The Pfizer-BioNTech shot has a recommended three-week (21-day) wait time between doses.
    • The Moderna shot has a recommended four-week (28-day) wait time in between doses.
  • The CDC advises getting the second dose as close to the recommended dates as possible. However, second doses are still considered valid if administered within a grace period of four days early and up to six weeks (42 days) after the first dose.

Is getting vaccinated safe?

  • According to Johns Hopkins Medicine, “the U.S. vaccine safety system works to make sure that all vaccines are as safe as possible.” All vaccines go through careful testing and continuous monitoring for problems and side effects. So far, no serious safety concerns have been reported about any of the three approved vaccines.
  • Those with common allergies, such as to certain foods or latex, can receive the COVID-19 vaccine, according to the CDC. Those who have had severe allergic reactions or any allergic reaction to a vaccine or other injection should contact their doctor before getting the vaccine, according to the CDC.

Am I eligible to be vaccinated?

  • Maryland is currently vaccinating those who fall into Phase 1C of the state’s vaccination plan. Phase 1C includes all adults 65 and older, plus:
    • All licensed, registered and certified health care providers, including front-line hospital staff.
    • Nursing home residents and staff.
    • People with intellectual and developmental disabilities.
    • People in assisted living, independent living, behavioral health and developmentally disabled group homes, and other shared housing facilities.
    • Law enforcement, firefighters, EMS, Correctional health care staff and officers and other public safety workers.
    • Teachers and staff in schools, including those in higher education, plus daycare providers.
    • Front-line judiciary staff.
    • Officials essential to the continuity of government operations.
    • Food and agriculture production, critical manufacturing, U.S. Postal Service, public mass transit and grocery store employees.
    • Veterinarians and support staff.
    • Clergy and other essential support for houses of worship.
    • Patients receiving hospital-based treatment, including in hospital outpatient centers, and diagnosed with at least one of the following conditions:
      • Cancer patients who are currently in active treatment.
      • End stage renal disease patients requiring hemodialysis.
      • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD).
      • Solid organ transplant recipients.
      • Sickle cell disease patients.
      • Diabetic patients (Type 1 and Type 2).

  • Yes. Maryland is currently in Phase 1C, but your county may be in a different phase. For example, Anne Arundel County is in Phase 1B. Where can I go to get a vaccine?

  • Maryland maintains a vaccine site locator map, but the sign-up process is different for every site.
  • The state runs three mass vaccination sites, and plans to open more in March. These sites are currently operating:
    • Six Flags in Bowie. Patients may sign up for an appointment online.
    • The Baltimore Convention Center. Patients must fill out a form to get in line for an appointment.
    • M&T Bank Stadium in Baltimore. Patients may sign up for an appointment online or by calling 1-855-634-6829.
  • Local jurisdictions are also running vaccine clinics for residents through their health departments. Most are encouraging citizens to sign up for waiting lists, even if they are not yet eligible for the vaccine under local guidelines.
    • Anne Arundel County.
    • Baltimore City.
    • Baltimore County.
    • Montgomery County.
    • Prince George's County.
  • Some pharmacies are authorized to vaccinate Maryland residents. In some cases, patients must sign up for an account with the pharmacy before searching for vaccine appointments.
    • CVS.
    • Giant.
    • Rite Aid.
    • Safeway.
    • Walgreens.
    • Walmart.
  • Hospitals are also offering vaccinations or running vaccination clinics across the state.

Should I sign up for multiple vaccine waitlists?

  • Currently, the state has a relatively decentralized distribution plan. Acting State Health Secretary Dennis R. Schrader told state lawmakers in February that residents should sign up for multiple waitlists, but take their names off other waitlists once they secure an appointment.
  • Maryland Gov. Larry Hogan announced that the state plans to create a statewide, centralized registration portal for its mass vaccination sites in March, but the website will not include vaccination sites such as pharmacies and hospitals.

Do I need to get vaccinated in the county where I live?

  • In most cases, you can get a vaccine if you live or work in a county. Maryland’s five largest jurisdictions — Baltimore City and Montgomery, Prince George’s, Baltimore and Anne Arundel counties — will vaccinate eligible patients who live or work within their borders.

Why is it so hard to get a vaccine?

  • Supply is not currently meeting demand. According to the Maryland Health Department, the federal government is giving Maryland 14,000 vaccine doses per day. More than 2 million Marylanders are eligible to receive the vaccine under Phase 1C of the state’s vaccination plan. Many vaccine recipients will require two doses.
  • Although the state’s vaccine plan is rolling, it may not wait for every single member of each group to be vaccinated. However, the majority of each phase’s group will be vaccinated eligibility is expanded, according to state guidelines.
  • According to the Vaccine Distribution Plan, the state estimates it will need 3 million doses of the vaccine before moving into Phase 2, in which more members of the public will be eligible to receive the vaccine.

What do I need to bring with me to my vaccine appointment?

  • According to the Maryland Department of Health, COVID-19 vaccine providers may ask you to bring documentation, such as a government-issued ID or any similar document, that proves you are in the correct eligible group for the phase that Maryland is vaccinating.
  • If obtaining a vaccine from a state-operated mass vaccination site without any documentation that proves eligibility, you will be required to sign an affidavit certifying under a penalty of perjury that you can get the COVID-19 vaccine at that moment in time.

How much will the vaccine cost?

  • According to the CDC, the federal government will cover the cost of COVID-19 vaccines and vaccinations.
  • According to the Maryland Insurance Administration, you cannot be charged for a COVID-19 vaccine. The Maryland Insurance Administration asks that if you are asked to pay for a vaccine, you call this number: 800-492-6116.
  • Those getting vaccinated should watch out for scammers, as incidents of unauthorized charges for COVID-19 vaccines and treatments have been reported.

How long will it take for the vaccine to make it safe to return to normal life?

  • According to the CDC, experts are not sure what percentage of people need to get the vaccine to achieve herd immunity.
  • Herd immunity is reached when enough people are protected — either because they contracted the disease and recovered, or because they were vaccinated — and spread becomes more difficult. This protects those who cannot be vaccinated, such as newborns.
  • The CDC and other experts are actively studying herd immunity for COVID-19 and plan to release information as it is available.
